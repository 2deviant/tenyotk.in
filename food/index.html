Nutrition vs Food

This page features an exploration into what I believe is the future of human nutrition. 80%-90% of my intake is in semi-liquid form: I put a bunch of nutritious stuff in a blender to save oodles of time, money, and health.

Good, Fast, Cheap…

… pick any two. Because most of us can’t afford a chef or two hours per day to procure the best ingredients and cook them, we resort to fast and cheap food. If we’re honest, most of us eat carcinogenic and diabetogenic crap on daily basis. What’s worse: we don’t eat enough of what's necessary to counteract the effects of the aforementioned crap.

What, Why, How?

What

The premise is simple: consume nutrition, not food.  Figure out your nutritional requirements, put them in a blender, turn it on. Sip on that super-smoothie during the day. Combined time and monetary cost is reduced by a factor of ten. Health benefits increased by about as comparable of a factor.

Why

Food, as we know it today, comes with many negative health consequences and it demands swaths of time most of us would rather spend doing something else. Food, like everything else, comes in three varieties:

  • [nutritionally] Good
  • Fast
  • Cheap

Pick two. In reality, people tend to overwhelmingly sacrifice on quality or — less so — on speed [of procurement, preparation, and consumption].  If one sacrifices the quality of one's food, one inevitably pays for it later in life with disease, hospital bills, and time in treatment.  If one sacrifices the time to engage in healthy eating, that time cannot be used for anything else.  In either case, one pays with time, the only true currency we have. Think of proper nutrition as a socioeconomic equalizer: less disease = more opportunities, more time = more opportunities, more time = [sometimes] more productivity.

How

You'll need a good blender. At least 2L capacity.

The Basics

  • Protein. Powder: whey, rice, pea, pumpkin, etc.
  • Fat. Avocado, olive oil, coconut oil.
  • Carbohydrates. Fruit: berries, bananas, mango, pineapple, etc.; all can be frozen, all cheap, all suitable for bulk purchase and long-term storage. It should be noted that adults do not require dietary carbohydrates at all.
  • Miscellaneous. Spinach and/or any leafy green to enhance the nutrient and fiber profile of the smoothie.
  • Supplements. Supplements such as daily multivitamin are necessary to ensure you don't accidentally deprive yourself of a key nutrient. Megadosing is bad.

Using the above-mentioned ingredients it is possible to design a smoothie for an entire day no matter one's nutritional requirements.

Palatability

It will take some experimentation to get such concoction to desired taste and texture. Thickening is typically achieved with fresh banana or avocado. Using hard vegetables and greens is not recommended for almost no blender will chop them up, thus leaving unpalatable chunks in your smoothies — kale being a notable example. Taste can be enhanced with vanilla extract, peanut butter, mango, and other volatile fruits.

Notes

Recovery

Eating before bed is generally considered to be detrimental to sleep quality. However, it is necessary for anyone undergoing intense physical activity during the day in order to prevent injury. Muscles, tendons, ligaments, bones mostly recover during sleep, having adequate energy and building material is therefore essential. The fact that the nutrition comes in liquid form eases the process somewhat. I typically finish my daily smoothie immediately before bed. On particularly heavy days, additional 30g of protein powder is taken.

Micronutrient Supplementation

Multivitamin supplementation is necessary due to the protocol's low ingredient diversity. Beware of multivitamin supplements containing horse-doses of some vitamins. Megadosing is bad. I take one multivitamin pill per day regardless of the stated Serving Size, which is usually more than one pill.

K1 and K2 are typically not present in multivitamin supplements, so they have to be taken separately. Aside from being prerequisites for blood-clotting, K2 is necessary for the bonding of calcium to the bones. Vitamin K deficiency leads to osteoporosis.

BAKED: Vitamins B-12, A, K, E, D are stored in the liver. This has two main implications:

  • Healthy individuals maintain a buffer of said micronutrients, affording them optimal function during the time of voluntary or involuntary fasting. All except B are fat-soluble, which allows them to be stored in fat as well. Lipolysis (fasting) releases those nutrients.
  • With the exception of B, overdosing on these vitamins is especially "bad" because they are not water-soluble, and thus cannot be easily flushed out of your system before reaching toxic levels.

General

I have one real meal about five days out of a week. I snack on real food — apples and nuts, for instance — about as often.

FAQ

I'm happy and healthy, why should I do this?

You're not. There's a 99.99% chance of you being malnourished. Not in sense of severe muscle loss, but in the sense of being deficient in at least one of the micronutrients necessary for long-term health.

Is this expensive?

No. Even if you eat McDonald's for all three meals, this method of nourishment is still cheaper.

Does this take al ot of time?

No. Takes at most fifteen minutes per day.

Is this compatible with my diet?

Yes. This method does not prescribe a particular diet, rather it shows how any given diet can be implemented at the absolutely lowest time and monetary cost, highest quality, and least harm.

Is it possible to go 100% liquid?

Possible, yes. Recommended, no.

  • In order to keep your jaw, teeth, and gums, periodic resistance stimulation of those areas is necessary.  Apples and nuts are perfect for this very function.
  • It is not advisable to completely decondition your body from processing solid food of questionable quality. You may get stuck in a forest (or Paris) for a while and have to survive by eating leaves, insects, and exotic animals.
  • There has been no credible, peer-reviewed research on the topic. It would be unwise to do this.

Can I just eat energy/protein/MRE bars?

No. Food bars are backups designed to supply you with nutrition in an emergency, such as hiking 50mi with a 100lb backpack or sitting in meetings all day.  Two main reasons a modern-day food bar is not a viable meal replacement as compared to its equivalent in powder/liquid form:

  • In order to keep it solid, a lot of stuff you don't want inside of you must be used. Most of the time: copious amounts of some form of sugar and stabilizers.
  • In contrast to powders/liquids, solid food lingers in the mouth much longer, requiring it to have a richer taste profile than its powder/liquid equivalent. Breathing while chewing pushes more of the food molecules through the nose thus enhancing the flavor, whether pleasant or foul. Think of it this way: would you enjoy a power bar if it tasted like tonic water or whiskey? A rare taste works in both liquid and solid forms: chocolate/cocoa/coffee is a notable exception. In order to make food bars palatable, a lot of stuff you don't want inside of you must be used: a combination of sugar, salt, and [bad] fat.

Can I subsist entirely on retail food replacement shakes/powders?

... when traveling, it is often one's goal to not experience projectile diarrhea or other consequences of inescapable, bad, local food...

Depends. Powder-based meal replacements such as Soylent, Huel, Ample, Ambronite, etc. are designed to be meal replacements as opposed to food bars, which are designed as supplements or emergency rations. That said, their micro- and macro-nutrient profiles center around a typical 1,500 - 2,000 calorie/day diet. If that is your caloric requirement, then the answer is most likely yes. Don't forget to munch on something hard every day to keep your teeth and jaw, and remember that this has not been studied by any credible scientific body.

If you're more like me — a 180lb male, strength-training five days per week, 1-2hr/day — your caloric requirement is around 4,000 calories/day on heavy weeks. Obtaining that energy from meal replacements will undoubtedly put you over the limit on most micro- and some macro-nutrients. Megadosing is bad. For instance, 4,000 cal from Soylent powder carries 200% micronutrient RDI, 130% saturated fat RDI, 140% sodium RDI, and 150g of sugar.

If your caloric intake is compatible with meal replacements’ nutrient profile, but you need significantly more protein, dilute the powder with raw/pure protein powder. The resulting concoction won’t taste as good as the original, but its boosted protein content won’t significantly elevate the caloric load of the resulting beverage.

Meal replacements serve as excellent post-workout meals.

Why Not Use Meal Preparation Services like Territory?

You can. In fact, I recommend services like Territory to everyone whom I coach on nutrition. These services have three main drawbacks:

  • They aren't cheap: $10-$15/meal.
  • You have little control over the quality of ingredients.
  • They [can] only exist in densely-populated areas, specifically cities.

Additionally, such services do not address nutrition intake scenarios of anyone who isn't parked near a refrigerator, and optionally a microwave oven while at work; e.g. peace officer, construction worker, currier. It is much easier to plop a hunk of ice into a bottle and carry it with you throughout the day.
